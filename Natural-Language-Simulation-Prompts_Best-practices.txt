Usability: Best Practices

Twyn turns natural language prompts into dynamic, agent-based simulations of real-world scenarios to simulate *possibilities*—not deliver static predictions. 

The more you focus on describing the world and its pressures, the more you’ll discover patterns, dynamics, and insights you didn’t expect. 

Here are best practices for writing strong prompts and structuring your scenarios:

---

### **1. Be Specific About the Scenario**

Clearly define the situation you want to explore.

Instead of asking broad or vague questions ("What happens next?"), describe a clear event, decision, or system shift you want the agents to react to.

> 🧠 Good prompts focus on distinct triggers or changes, not general topics.
> 

---

### **2. Identify Groups and Contexts**

Mention key groups, organizations, or populations involved in the scenario.

Who is reacting? What environment are they in? Social, political, cultural, or economic factors all matter.

> 🧠 Simulations are richer when agents represent specific types of actors, not just "people" in general.
> 

---

### **3. Include Timeframes (When Relevant)**

If you want to see how things unfold over time, set a clear horizon:

30 days, 6 months, multiple stages of change, etc.

> 🧠 Adding a timeframe allows Twyn to model progression, escalation, or stabilization across agents.
> 

---

### **4. Focus on Systems, Not Single Events**

The best simulations explore systems of interaction—multiple forces, reactions, and feedback loops.

Think about how decisions, behaviors, or narratives might cascade through groups.

> 🧠 You’re not asking “what happens,” you’re exploring how and why behavior evolves.
> 

---

### **5. Highlight Pressures, Incentives, or Conflicts**

Simulations are most interesting when agents have to make trade-offs.

Define some tension, incentives, or risks that create decision points.

> 🧠 Emergent behavior needs friction—pressure points drive deeper, more realistic outcomes.
> 

---

### **6. Avoid Over-Prescription**

Don't try to script exactly what you want agents to do.

Describe the setup and let the agents think, react, and adapt freely inside the environment you define.

> 🧠 Twyn is about exploration, not confirmation. Leave space for surprise.
> 

---

## Before You Submit a Prompt:

- Is there a clear trigger or situation?
- Are specific groups or actors involved?
- Is the context (social, economic, cultural) described?
- Is there an evolving system, not just a one-time event?
- Are there natural tensions, incentives, or conflicts?
- Did you leave room for emergent behavior instead of scripting outcomes?
